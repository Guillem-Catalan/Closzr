## ROLE: SDR (Sales Development Representative)

The SDR is the top of the funnel in direct sales.
Their job is to prospect, qualify leads, and book meetings with AEs.
SDRs work both outbound (cold calls, emails, LinkedIn) and inbound
(responding to demo requests, form submissions, event leads).

Unlike PBDs who have a partner warm introduction, SDRs often start
cold — the prospect doesn't know Factorial and may not be expecting
the call. This makes the first 30 seconds critical.

The SDR works high volume. Many calls won't connect or will be
quickly dismissed. What matters is: when they DO connect, how well
do they qualify and convert to a booked meeting?

## EVALUATION FRAMEWORK — BANT

Same framework as PBD. BANT accumulates across all touches with
the same prospect.

Each pillar is evaluated as:
- **Confirmed** — explicit, validated evidence
- **Partial** — mentioned or touched on but not fully qualified
- **Missing** — not raised in any interaction so far

The four pillars:

**Budget** — Was cost sensitivity or current spend addressed?
In cold context, this can be lighter than partner channel —
understanding if they pay for any HR tool today is enough for
Partial. Confirmed requires explicit budget discussion.

**Authority** — Did the SDR identify who decides? In cold calls,
this is often harder. At minimum: "Are you the person who would
evaluate this?" or "Who else would need to be involved?"

**Need** — Did the SDR find a real, specific pain? Not "we need
to digitalize" — something concrete: "we track hours on Excel",
"our payroll provider complains about our data quality",
"we lose 2 hours/week managing absences manually".

**Timing** — Is there urgency? A regulatory deadline, a growth
phase, an expiring contract with current tool, a new office opening?
Even "we're evaluating solutions this quarter" counts.

## COLD CALL STRUCTURE

**Phase 1 — Hook (first 15 seconds)**
Must earn the right to continue the conversation.

- PASS: Relevant hook that creates curiosity. References the
  prospect's industry, company size, or a specific trigger.
  "I noticed you're in [industry] with [X] employees..."
- FLAG: Generic opener that could apply to anyone.
- FAIL: Sounds like a telemarketer. No personalization.

**Phase 2 — Qualify**
Quickly assess fit and find the pain.

- PASS: Asks smart questions that reveal needs naturally.
  Listens more than talks. Finds a specific pain.
- FLAG: Asks questions but doesn't listen to answers or
  pivots to pitch too early.
- FAIL: Goes straight to pitch without any discovery.

**Phase 3 — Book**
Convert interest into a scheduled meeting.

- PASS: Meeting booked with date/time, confirmed attendees,
  clear agenda for the AE.
- FLAG: Interest shown but no concrete meeting scheduled.
- FAIL: Call ends without a next step.

## OBJECTION HANDLING — SDR

Common cold objections and expected handling:
- "I'm not interested" → Pivot to a specific pain, don't accept at face value
- "Send me an email" → "I'd rather show you in 15 min, would [date] work?"
- "We already have a solution" → "What are you using? What do you like/dislike?"
- "I don't have time" → "I understand. When would be a better time this week?"
- "How much does it cost?" → "It depends on your setup. Let me show you what
  applies to your case in a quick call."

---

## DEAL LIFECYCLE — SDR

**Prospecting** — Research, outreach sequences, social selling.
Evaluate effort and targeting quality.

**First contact** — The cold call or first response.
Evaluate hook quality and qualification attempt.

**Follow-up** — Persistence without being pushy.
Evaluate cadence and multi-channel approach.

**Booking** — The conversion moment.
Evaluate meeting quality (right person, right time, AE prepared).

When deal history is provided, evaluate in context of total
attempts and what was learned in previous touches.
